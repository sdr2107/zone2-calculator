How to Measure Your Resting Heart Rate

Your resting heart rate (RHR) is the number of times your heart beats per minute when you are completely at rest. It is one of the most accessible indicators of cardiovascular fitness — a lower RHR generally reflects a stronger, more efficient heart.

Best Method

Measure first thing in the morning before getting out of bed. Lie still for 60 seconds, then count your pulse at your wrist (radial artery) or neck (carotid artery) for a full 60 seconds. Repeat on 3 consecutive days and average the values.

Using a Smartwatch or HR Monitor

Most modern smartwatches measure RHR automatically during sleep or rest. These give good estimates. Look for a "resting HR" or "morning HR" reading in your app. Wear the watch snugly to improve optical sensor accuracy.

Normal resting HR for adults is 60–100 bpm. A well-trained endurance athlete may have an RHR of 40–50 bpm. A high RHR (>90 bpm consistently) may warrant a conversation with your doctor.

How to Measure Your Maximum Heart Rate

Your maximum heart rate (HRmax) is the highest number of beats per minute your heart can achieve during maximal exertion. It declines slightly with age and varies significantly between individuals.

Tanaka Formula (Default — Age-Based Estimate)

HRmax = 208 − (0.7 × age)
This is the most validated age-based formula (Tanaka et al., JACC 2001). It is more accurate than the older 220 − age formula, particularly for older and fitter adults. It is a population average — your actual HRmax may differ by ±10–15 bpm.

Measured Value (More Accurate)

A true HRmax can be measured during a maximal exercise test — typically a graded treadmill or cycle ergometer test supervised by a sports physician or cardiologist. During an all-out 2–3 minute effort (e.g. a hard sprint finish in a race), your heart rate may also approach true HRmax. Use the highest HR value you have reliably recorded during intense exercise as an approximation.

Why HRR Method — Not % HRmax?

There are two common ways to calculate heart rate training zones. The HRR method is recommended for most people because it accounts for how fit you are at baseline.

% HRmax Method

Zone 2 = 64–76% of your maximum heart rate. Simple and widely used. Does not account for your resting heart rate — so two people with very different fitness levels get identical zones.

HRR Method (Karvonen)

Zone 2 = Resting HR + 40–60% of your Heart Rate Reserve. Uses your actual working range of heart rate. A fitter person with a lower resting HR gets a wider, appropriately calibrated zone.

Recommended
HRR = HRmax − Resting HR
Zone 2 lower = Resting HR + (40% × HRR)
Zone 2 upper = Resting HR + (60% × HRR)

Example: Two people, both with HRmax 180. Person A has a resting HR of 40 bpm (well-trained); Person B has 75 bpm (sedentary). The % HRmax method gives both the same Zone 2: 115–137 bpm. The HRR method correctly gives Person A: 112–148 bpm and Person B: 102–129 bpm — reflecting their very different physiology.

The HRR method is not perfect — the only way to confirm true metabolic thresholds is a laboratory lactate or gas exchange test. But as a practical field method for setting training targets, HRR is meaningfully more accurate than % HRmax.
